How Much Water Should a New Dad Actually Drink?

By Tom | February 2026 | 5 min read

you know the standard advice. "drink 8 glasses of water a day." except nobody tells you what happens to your hydration when you're running on 4 hours of broken sleep and mainlining coffee to survive.

short answer: it gets worse. a lot worse. here's why, and what to actually do about it.

Sleep deprivation and dehydration are linked

a 2019 study in the journal Sleep looked at data from over 20,000 adults across the US and China. people who regularly slept 6 hours or less were significantly more dehydrated than those sleeping 8 hours.

the reason is a hormone called vasopressin. your body releases more of it during the later stages of sleep, and vasopressin tells your kidneys to retain water. if you're waking up every 2 hours to a crying baby, those later sleep stages get cut short. your kidneys flush more water than they should. you wake up dehydrated even if you drank plenty the day before.

How much water do you actually need?

the NHS recommends 6-8 glasses per day (roughly 1.5-2 litres). the European Food Safety Authority says 2.5 litres for adult men, including water from food. most guidelines land somewhere around 2-3 litres of total fluid per day.

but if you're sleep deprived, you probably need more. there's no exact formula, but a reasonable approach is:

  • baseline: 2 litres of plain water per day
  • add 250ml for every coffee or caffeinated drink (caffeine is a mild diuretic)
  • add 500ml on days where you slept less than 5 hours
  • front-load it: drink a full glass of water first thing in the morning, before coffee

Does coffee count?

sort of. coffee does contain water, and moderate coffee consumption (3-4 cups) doesn't cause net dehydration in habitual drinkers. a 2014 study in PLOS ONE found that regular coffee drinkers weren't more dehydrated than water drinkers at the same volume.

but there's a catch for new dads. you're probably drinking more coffee than usual. and above 4-5 cups, the diuretic effect starts to outweigh the hydration. plus, the caffeine after 2pm wrecks whatever sleep opportunity you do get.

a practical rule: match every coffee with a glass of water. not because coffee dehydrates you directly, but because it's an easy habit that keeps your total intake up. and it makes you think twice about that fifth cup.

Signs you're not drinking enough

the obvious one is urine colour. dark yellow = not enough. pale straw = fine. clear = you might be overdoing it.

less obvious signs that new dads often miss:

  • headaches. that 3pm headache might not be stress. it might be dehydration.
  • brain fog. even mild dehydration (1-2% body water loss) impairs cognitive function. when you're already foggy from sleep deprivation, dehydration makes it significantly worse.
  • irritability. yes, dehydration makes you more irritable. combine that with sleep deprivation and a screaming baby, and you've got a rough afternoon.
  • fatigue. feeling exhausted is normal as a new dad. but some of it is fixable. proper hydration won't replace sleep, but it can take the edge off.

Making it actually happen

knowing you should drink more water is the easy part. actually doing it when you're holding a baby with one hand and trying to make a bottle with the other is harder.

things that actually worked for me:

  • keep a bottle everywhere. bedside table, kitchen counter, changing station. if it's within reach, you'll drink it.
  • drink before feeds. baby feeds every 2-3 hours. use that as your reminder. every time the baby eats, you drink.
  • morning glass first. before you even think about coffee. full glass of water. it's the single highest-impact habit.
  • track it. even roughly. DadFuel lets you tap once per glass. takes 2 seconds. the tracking itself creates awareness.

a note on over-hydration: it's rare, but drinking too much water too fast can be dangerous (hyponatraemia). if you're drinking 4+ litres a day and feeling dizzy or nauseous, ease off. the goal is consistent, moderate intake throughout the day.
